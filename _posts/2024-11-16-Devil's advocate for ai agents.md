Ever since Spring 2024, the talk has been about agents. While I do agree that agentic workflows are a way to help us accomplish more complicated, multi-step processes via langauge models, I'm not certain of the place ai agents has in our future. Agents are programs that can perceive environmental cues and take actions to achieve specific goals. Yet their defining characteristics-autonomy, perception, decision making, task completion-can be found lots of tech products before the arrival of gpt (thermostats, chatbots, home assistants). Hence, I feel like ai agents are perhaps a marketing scheme to add more spark to the diminishing appeal of "customized gpts" and a "gptstore". 
However, I'm not against agents. Rather than having an arsenal of AI agents to complete each of your tasks, I think a more likely scenario would be one in which a central agent (similar to Samantha from the Movie her) acquires new skills based on the needs and desires of the user. In practice, this would mean developing standard operating procedures for repetitive tasks based on human instruction (and human in the loop intervention for unexpected cases), and function calling / tool use for acquiring different skill trees. Ultimately the value of such a tool is not in the number of tasks it's able to half adeptly do, but rather the level of understanding towards the user and reliability in assigned tasks. In other words, predictive analytics would still be a central theme, going on top of it, flawless execution on repetitive everyday tasks.
